British Reparations

Slavery in Britain had been very much tied to the trade aspect of it. The Trans Atlantic Slave Trade had benefitted Britain greatly in profits as they pushed people out of Africa and forced them mostly westwards. While America was a large part of it, they had also dealt with the Caribbean. Indeed, the Caribbean was a large destination for many slaveholders to engage in the trade as well as a place for many to force them into labor.

Slavery in the Caribbean was just as horrifying as it was in America. One of the main differences was the mindset of slaveholders. Often times slaves were seen as replaceable through trade, so they were not often taken care of and weren’t forced to make children amongst themselves. They were treated with less care as they could simply buy more. They had forced them to work on cash crop fields to create massive profits for the slaveholders, many times all the way back in Britain. Indeed, the slaveholders had lived away from their plantations and were less involved with their slaves. These differences paint a different picture than compared to American slavery and with it comes its own consequences of the state of slaves and their conditions after slavery had been abolished, shaping demands for reparations in a different way.

Protests in the Caribbean. Black Caribbeans look for compensation for injustices.

Indeed, one of the most significant points of debate for British/Caribbean reparations is the immediate results of the abolition of slavery in 1834. The Slave Compensation Act 1837 was essentially paying back former slaveholders. It can be seen in the document Accounts of slave compensation claims, that in fact former British slaveholders in many British territories, were compensated for their slaves when slavery was abolished.¹ They were paid with money by the British government for the “property” they had lost. This is further emphasized by the fact that the former enslaved themselves were not given any compensation. In fact, many of the former enslaved of British slaveholders had undergone apprenticeships after they had gained their freedom. Much like the sharecropping abuse in America, former slaves “worked as before for their former owner, for three-quarters of their time, and could work for others for the rest of the week and receive a small wage. This period was supposed to get the ex-slaves prepared for their freedom in eight years' time at the end of the apprenticeship” (Discovering Bristol). This system of apprenticeship was seen as another form of slavery even after its former abolition, to the point that this system was abolished in 1838.

Many similarities to America can be drawn from this whilst also spotting differences. Regardless, the idea for demanding reparations remain–the generations of the former enslaved want compensation for the outcomes that slavery has instilled into society. Britain's Black Debt: Reparations for Caribbean Slavery and Native Genocide by Hilary McD. Beckles gives a general example of this, stating “Caribbean indigenous communities, still caught in the grip of British genocidal policies, and blacks who have remained deeply economically disenfranchised and racially targeted as a subordinate ethnicity by racist ideology, suffered considerable harm within the slavery and post-slavery systems maintained by Britain”.² Beckles suggests that in the Caribbean, one of slavery’s effects was the racist philosophies placed upon society that had further wronged Black people within the Caribbean. These reparations then, are to undo these philosophies as well. The idea of “subordinate ethnicity” plagues societies to this day and is what is demanded.

These wrongdoings that Beckles outlines can be seen within the goals of organizations such as The Caribbean Community (CARICOM) which formed to seek these reparations. CARICOM’s 10-point plan is very similar to the demands that American organizations look for. Within their demands, they particularly lean on education and its importance of it. Whether it be to create “African Knowledge Programs” or “Cultural Institutions” they aim to have everyone, former slave families, former slaveholder families, and those who were not directly involved, to be educated on the subject as well as educated on what it means to be African.³
